# PPP Logo Test — Reference

Expanded rationale and examples from David Bain's Pencil-Pen-Pigment framework. Read this when you need deeper context beyond the core skill checklist.

## Why effectiveness beats attractiveness

It is important not to become attached to a logo simply because it looks good. An effective logo is more valuable than a pretty one. Given the choice between an ineffective, attractive logo and an effective, unattractive logo, choose the latter.

An effective logo is easy to identify regardless of the medium.

## Mediums a logo must survive

Typical applications:

- Websites
- Social media
- Product packaging
- Print advertising
- Email marketing
- In-store displays
- Branded promotional products (USB drives, pens, glasses, bags, umbrellas, etc.)
- Influencer marketing
- Events and expos
- Embroidery on caps and shirts

Some brands also need letterheads, vehicle wraps, or large-scale structural branding (e.g. stadiums).

## The three tests (detail)

### Pencil Test — easy to draw

If the mark cannot be sketched simply, it will struggle in low-fidelity contexts: whiteboards, napkins, embroidery digitising, favicon reduction, and memory recall.

### Pen Test — recognisable on a pen

The side of a pen is a proxy for extreme horizontal compression and tiny reproduction. Marks that only work large will fail here.

### Pigment Test — single colour

If identity collapses without a multicolour treatment, the logo will fail in fax-era constraints, single-ink print, engraving, embossing, and many promo products.

## Brands evolve toward effectiveness

### KFC

Early KFC marks were less effective by PPP standards. Over time the logo evolved toward clearer, more reproducible forms.

Source for historical marks: https://logos-world.net/kfc-logo/

### Microsoft

Microsoft generally passed the pigment test early (important for printed manuals) and later moved toward forms more likely to pass pencil and pen tests.

### Apple (1976)

The detailed 1976 Apple mark is a reminder that many large brands started with non-PPP logos and refined later.

## Wordmark vs symbol

### Prefer wordmarks for young companies

A **wordmark** (logotype) is the brand name set as the logo. Examples often cited: IBM, FedEx, Visa, ASOS wordmark.

A **symbol** is an abstract or simplified visual stand-in for the brand. Target's bullseye is a classic highly recognisable symbol.

### Examples

- **FedEx** — bold wordmark; purple/orange with a hidden arrow in the letterforms
- **Visa** — distinctive sans wordmark; blue/yellow scheme
- **ASOS** — clean wordmark plus a separate symbol used on social avatars

Symbols earn their keep through recognition. Until then, the name is the asset.

## Dark and light backgrounds

An effective logo must read on both dark and light fields.

- Some brands use **badges** (common in food/CPG) so the mark sits on a controlled shape over any background — e.g. Chicco
- Others ship **light and dark logo variants** in brand guidelines — e.g. Samsung white mark for dark backgrounds

## Printable colour

Screens use RGB light; printers use CMYK pigment. Not every screen colour prints cleanly. Embroidery adds another constraint: available thread colours.

Design the identity so structure survives when colour is reduced or substituted.

## Logo variation system

| Variation | Purpose |
|-----------|---------|
| Full-color | Digital and print where colour reproduction is possible |
| One-color | Limited colour options or black-and-white print |
| Monochrome | Single colour needs such as embroidery or engraving |
| Reversed | Dark backgrounds where the standard mark fails |
| Simplified | Small or low-resolution screens/print |
| Stacked | Vertical or narrow spaces |
| Icon | Standalone symbol for social profiles and app icons |

Consistency across variations matters more than forcing one file to do every job.

## Breaking the rules

Once you understand *why* these rules exist, break them when context demands it.

## Final note for existing brands

If you already shipped a non-PPP logo (labels, brochures, billboards), that is common. Many billion-dollar brands started that way. From a business perspective, building something people pay for matters more than perfecting the mark on day one. You can update the logo later.
